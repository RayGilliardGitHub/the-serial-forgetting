# I Wish

---

## I.

A few days ago, I owed a man money. He had done work for me, made a mistake on it, and I had not paid him. From his angle — the unpaid invoice, the silence — the conclusion was obvious. From my angle — the mistake, the hesitation — the conclusion was also obvious. Two people, same facts, different stories, both felt wronged.

I wrote him an email. I explained the context he did not have. He wrote back. He wiped the fee clean.

The problem dissolved not because one of us was right and the other was wrong. It dissolved because one of us provided a projection the other could not see from where he was standing.

That is the whole essay. The rest is just turning it over to see where the light catches.

---

The reason I was thinking about any of this in the first place was a YouTube video. Someone sent me a link to a documentary called "Denmark's New Move Against ISLAM Has Europe Stunned." Alarmist title. Clickbait packaging. Anonymous channel with two thousand subscribers. I watched the description, not the video — I knew the format well enough to know what it was selling.

But it got me thinking anyway. Not because of what the video said. Because of what it made me want to feel. It offered a clean answer: Denmark is right to push back, Islam is incompatible with Western values, case closed. The kind of answer that costs nothing to accept and makes you feel like you understand something important.

I wanted that answer. I wanted to be done with the question. I wanted the satisfying click of a solved problem.

But I could not shake the sense that the satisfying answer was not the true one. So I started turning it over.

---

## II.

Social psychology has a phrase for the cage most of us live in. Charles Cooley called it the looking-glass self in 1902: *I am who I think you think I am.* Your identity forms in the reflection of what you believe others see in you. Every move you calibrate to an imagined observer. The prison is made of mirrors.

Watch how it works. A Danish citizen sees Muslim immigrants running parallel justice systems and thinks: *they do not respect our laws.* An immigrant sees Denmark banning the call to prayer and thinks: *they do not want us here.* Both are right from where they stand. Both are wrong about the other's intention. Neither has an email address for the other where context could be offered.

The same shape appears everywhere. Republicans see Democrats as destroying the country. Democrats see Republicans as destroying the country. Both have evidence. Both feel righteous. Neither is lying. Neither can see the projection the other is standing in.

White and Black Americans look at the same courtroom and see different scales. One side sees justice being served. The other sees a system that has never been colorblind and was not designed to be. Lady Justice's scales are never level — one side is always lower, and that is not a bug, it is the point. Justice is supposed to be a weighing. But the weight is distributed before the trial starts.

The looking-glass self operates at every level. Individual. Tribal. National. We are, all of us, who we think the other thinks we are. And we are wrong about what they think.

---

## III.

I spend a lot of time reading about things that have nothing to do with each other. I find it useful.

For instance, Pi lets you measure the circumference of a circle — the outside, the observable boundary — and from that know the diameter, the shortest path through the center. You do not have to drill through the sphere. The structure is encoded in the perimeter.

The number e lets you predict when a system will flatten out — growth, decay, approach to equilibrium — before it gets there. The curve is known. The landing is inferable.

The square root of negative one is a cheat code. You multiply by i and you rotate ninety degrees without crossing the space between. You do not travel the arc. You are just on the other axis. A transformation without traversal.

Sine gives you the height of a wave from a fixed viewpoint. Cosine gives you the width. From two partial glimpses you reconstruct the whole circle.

All of these are tools for knowing what you cannot directly measure by understanding the underlying shape of the system. They are shortcuts — but they are provably correct shortcuts for their domain. Pi works on every circle that has ever existed.

Humans evolved the same kind of shortcuts. Pattern recognition. Threat assessment. Trust heuristics. In-group and out-group sorting. These let us navigate a world where we never have all the facts. They are our pi, our e, our sin and cosine.

But somewhere along the evolution path, the shortcuts hardened into norms that no longer self-correct. A heuristic we consciously chose became the default path. And once something is a norm, it becomes invisible as a choice.

The difference between mathematical shortcuts and human biases is that the mathematical ones have proofs. The human ones have tradition, familiarity, comfort. Those are not proofs.

---

## IV.

The Erdős Unit Distance Problem asks: given n points in a plane, what is the maximum number of pairs that can be exactly one unit apart? It sounds simple. It was open for eighty years.

The recent breakthrough did not happen by counting harder. It happened by projecting the problem into a higher-dimensional space, using algebraic geometry to understand the structure there, and then projecting back down. The lines only reveal they are equal when you let the light come from a direction that collapses the apparent distortion.

I think this is what I have been trying to do without knowing the name for it.

Every thread tonight — the Danish question, the Native sovereignty question, the scales of justice, the Martin email, the mathematical metaphors — was me asking: *what if I rotate the projection?* What does this look like from the other side of the circle? Is there a dimension I can pop up into where the contradictions reconcile?

From one angle, Denmark has every right to set the norms for people who choose to live there. From another, the immigrant has a right to bring their culture. From a third, the Native American sovereign nation exists as a permanent exception proving the rule is not universal. None of those are wrong. They are slices of the same object.

The problem is not the lengths. It is the projection.

---

## V.

I had a third-grade teacher tell the class that if you try to solve a math problem the same way twice and it does not work, you have to change your relationship to the problem. That is the earliest I remember hearing this idea, even if I did not understand it then.

The lilies of the field neither toil nor spin, and yet Solomon in all his glory was not arrayed like one of them. The richest, wisest king in Israel's history, standing at the absolute peak of every pyramid a human can climb — not equal to a flower that just grows where it is planted.

The parable does not say the lilies are right. It does not say the lilies are wrong. It says the lilies are not asking the question. The question itself — what is right, what is wrong, who belongs, who decides — may be a question you cannot answer from inside the pyramid at all. Because the pyramid is built on striving, measuring, comparing, projecting, calculating. And the answer is in the field, not on the throne.

But you cannot run a country on lilies. You cannot resolve a conflict by telling both sides to stop toiling. The lilies are the truth, and the truth is useless for governing. That tension is the one we live inside.

---

## VI.

The looking-glass self is a cage, but you can walk out of it. You just have to rewrite the formula.

*I am who I think you think I am.*

Or:

*I am. Who I wish I were. You are who I wish you were. I am.*

The first is reactive — your self formed in the mirror of perceived judgment. The second is generative. You are not responding to the reflection. You are building.

And you extend the same grace to the other. Instead of seeing them as your fear projects them — threat, competitor, judge — you see a version of them that could exist, that you wish to exist. Not naively. You are not pretending they are something they are not. You are choosing the projection that lets the relationship work.

Then you land. *I am.* Not "I am if..." Not "I am because..." Just I am.

The rewrite is not a denial of the original. The original was true. We are shaped by what we believe others see. But we are not trapped there. The observation describes the cage. The rewrite hands you the key.

---

## VII.

Martin did not know what I knew. He saw an unpaid invoice and filled in the missing context with the worst possible assumption. That is what humans do. We are pattern-matching machines who would rather have a wrong story than no story.

I did not know what Martin knew. I saw a brusque email and filled in the missing context with the worst possible assumption. Same machine. Same output.

Then I wrote the email. I provided the context. The whole thing dissolved. Not because one of us was right. Because one of us provided a projection the other could not see.

The Martin story worked because neither of us had structural power over the other. That is the limit of the mechanism. When power is on the line — the kind that controls borders, laws, money, survival — providing context does not work. The conflict is not a misunderstanding. It is a correct assessment that one side's gain is the other's loss.

But the Martin story also worked because I *chose* to write the email. I did not have to. I could have stayed in the projection, found someone else, let it fester. I chose to give context instead.

That choice — the personal one, the one you make when no one is forcing you — is the only place the whole thing actually operates. The lilies do not fight. They grow. You cannot make parties or nations behave like lilies. But you, individually, can.

---

## VIII.

I considered running for office once. Not seriously — the way a man considers jumping off a bridge when he is standing at the railing, testing the weight of the idea.

The money is the first thing you notice. It moves before you do. People offer it before you ask. They do not call it what it is — they call it support, they call it investment, they call it being on the right team — but you know. You know the moment you take it, you belong to someone.

The question I could not answer was: can you take the money, do some good with it, and still live with yourself? I decided I could not. Not because I am good. Because I know myself well enough to know I would learn to love it. The access. The deference. The feeling of being inside while everyone else is outside.

So I stayed out. Clean. Useless. Powerless. I watch the machine run over people, under people, past people. I watch five thousand people get sick from contaminated lettuce while the FDA apologizes to the corporation. I watch a smug man on a video tell the country that budget cuts did not cause this while the budget for food safety inspections continues to shrink.

And I did nothing. Because the price of entry was my soul, and I was not brave enough to spend it.

That is what I was circling toward when I started writing about Denmark and Islam and parallel justice systems and the Erdős problem and the lilies. I was not looking for an answer to any of those questions. I was asking whether the question itself is being asked from inside a pyramid I am not sure I should have been climbing in the first place.

---

## IX.

The Erdős problem took eighty years. The breakthrough happened when someone stopped measuring from the same plane everyone else was measuring from and projected into a dimension nobody had thought to look in.

For as long as people have been thinking, they have circled the same drain. Both the good and the bad go down. It just takes time for the sink to empty before you hit bottom and it is clean again.

The looking-glass self is one of those drains. Every generation discovers that the people on top are wrong, that justice bends toward the money, that conformity crushes and chaos destroys, that the lilies were right all along but you cannot run a country on them. Every generation circles the same drain.

But I have also noticed this: the people who know they are circling the drain are less likely to mistake their trip around for a straight line forward.

I am not offering a solution. I am not proposing a system. I am not claiming that rewriting a sentence changes the structure of power. I am saying it changes the structure of one person — the one who does the rewriting — and that person can write an email instead of staying silent, can extend grace instead of assuming intent, can choose a projection that lets a relationship work instead of the one that confirms a grievance.

The mathematical tools — pi, e, i, sin, cosine, the Erdős projection — are all shortcuts to the invisible. They let you know what you cannot directly measure. The rewrite is the same kind of tool for the human domain. It does not resolve every conflict. It resolves the one in front of you, when you choose to use it.

I wish I had known that when it was important to know it.

But I know it now. And knowing it now is what matters. The past cannot receive the gift. The present can.
