**Headline / Hook:**

I think I figured out why the galaxy is silent — and it starts with a Karen in a grocery store.

**Body:**

Four acronyms kept appearing in my head whenever I tried to understand why things fall apart:

• FOMO (Fear Of Missing Out)
• NIMBY (Not In My Back Yard)
• FAFO (F\*ck Around, Find Out)
• HILO (History Is the Loudest Opinion)

At first I thought they were just social phenomena. Descriptions of a certain kind of person. Easy to mock, hard to change.

Then I noticed the same four-stage sequence showing up everywhere.

Quantum decoherence. The collapse of the Roman Republic. The extinction of the dinosaurs. The arc of a failing marriage. The life cycle of a startup. The silence of the cosmos.

The pattern is always the same:

Every complex system starts in a coherent state — parts aligned, energy flowing, remembering itself without effort. Then it forgets to maintain that coherence. Comparison creeps in. Territory gets guarded. The collision becomes inevitable. And after the dust settles, the survivors rewrite the story.

The Hindu Yugas described it. Buddha diagnosed it. Confucius prescribed for it. Jesus named it. The Stoics trained against it. Zera Yacob reasoned his way to it in an Ethiopian cave in 1667. They all arrived at the same place independently because the pattern isn't cultural — it's structural.

I wrote an essay about it. It's called **The Serial Forgetting**.

It's not a scientific paper. It's a lens — an offer to see a pattern that, once noticed, is difficult to unsee.

The essay's real argument isn't about civilizations. It's about maintenance. The invisible work required to keep anything good alive — relationships, institutions, character, attention, truth.

The asteroid was punctuation. The forgetting was the sentence.
