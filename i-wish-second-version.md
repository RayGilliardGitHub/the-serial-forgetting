# I Wish (Second Version)

Nancy and I were married for 32 years. She died of cancer. I am still here.

That is the fact underneath every essay I have written, including the first version of this one. I danced around it with pi and the Erdős problem and the lilies of the field. I used Denmark as a decoy. I projected my grief onto civilization and called it philosophy. I hid behind intelligence because that was the only thing I knew how to do.

She was a real writer. She had a talent I will never hold a candle to. I am not being modest. It is simply true. She could do things with language that I cannot do. I am still trying to learn from her, and she has been gone long enough that her voice in my head is starting to fade. That terrifies me.

Nancy used to remind me of the plan. All the time. Work, school, real job, marriage, kids, work, retirement. That was what I had built. A sequence. A system. A life I could execute like a project plan. She would say it back to me when I got lost in the details, and I always knew what she meant. The plan was not the point. The plan was the container. She was the content.

Now the container is intact and the content is gone.

I am sixty-seven years old. I have the plan still — the same one I have been running my whole life. But nobody is reminding me what it is for. Nobody is standing on the other side of it. I keep executing steps and arriving at destinations that were supposed to be shared, and I am the only one there.

The first version of this essay ended with "The past cannot receive the gift. The present can." That is true. That sentence took me four essays to earn the right to write. I meant it. I still mean it. But I did not say what the gift was, because I was afraid of naming it.

The gift is her. The gift was her. The gift was every morning I woke up next to someone who knew the whole shape of me and stayed anyway. The gift was being seen for thirty-two years by someone whose judgment I trusted absolutely. The gift was having a plan and having someone who made the plan worth executing.

And then she died. And the plan became a list of things I do alone. And I have been trying to fill the hole with everything except what I actually need, because what I actually need is not available.

I do not know how to live without her. I have been trying to figure it out by writing essays. That is the honest truth. Every page I have written since she died has been an attempt to find a living plan — a way to be in the world that does not require her presence, because her presence is no longer available.

So far I have not found it. But I have found something else.

I found out that I cannot think my way through this. I cannot project my way out of it. I cannot rotate the angle far enough to see a dimension where she is still here. The Erdős trick does not work on grief. There is no higher-dimensional space where cancer was not real and thirty-two years did not happen and I am not alone in a house that was built for two.

I wish I had known that when it was important to know it.

But I know it now.

And knowing it now is what matters.

The past cannot receive the gift.

The present can.

*— Raymond Gilliard*
*— July 20, 2026*
